The Methodology

Digital Brain Surgery

After Week 3 testing, the bot exists and has real feedback from real users. Live Brain Surgery addresses the most realistic scenario: the bot works, but specific behaviors are wrong. We don't rebuild. We operate. The Week 3 testing log is the patient chart. The prompt is the patient.

📋
No Data, No Surgery
Week 4 runs on Week 3 evidence. A surgeon doesn't operate without a patient chart. If the testing log is incomplete — vague, missing inputs and outputs, no Like/Dislike distinction — the session cannot proceed. Garbage in, garbage out.
🎯
Targeted, Not Wholesale
We don't rebuild the bot from scratch. We identify the specific instruction producing the wrong behavior and operate on that section only. Precision over destruction. A good surgeon doesn't replace the whole organ when only one valve is failing.
✋
Confirm Before You Cut
After diagnosis, the surgeon proposes the fix. Nothing is touched until the Super User confirms the plan. This is not optional. An unconfirmed surgery on a live system is how you create new problems while fixing old ones.
Before Week 4 Begins

What the Surgeon Needs

The Week 3 testing log must be complete before Week 4 begins. These four fields are the minimum required to operate. Missing fields mean the surgeon is guessing — and surgeons don't guess.

Required: Structured Testing Data from Week 3
Every row in the testing log must have all four fields populated. Incomplete rows are not usable. One row per test scenario — no combining multiple issues into a single entry.
FIELD 1
Like / Dislike
The gut reaction. Good or bad. This is the tester's verdict before any analysis.
FIELD 2
Input & Output
Exact text — what was typed, what came back. Word for word. This is the evidence.
FIELD 3
Why
One sentence. What made it good or bad. A reason without evidence is a guess.
FIELD 4
How It Should Behave
If the tester knows the correct output — describe it. Like ordering a steak: be specific. The surgeon needs the order, not just the complaint.
The Operating Sequence

Live Brain Surgery — 5 Steps

Every session follows this sequence. No shortcuts, no reordering. The Super User drives. The AI operates under guidance.

Step 01
🩺
Symptoms
Upload the Week 3 testing log. The AI reads all tester entries — every Like/Dislike, every input/output pair, every "why." Patterns across multiple testers surface first. One tester's complaint is noise. Three testers flagging the same behavior is a symptom.
Read the patient chart
›
Step 02
🔬
Diagnose
Identify the root cause inside the prompt. Which specific instruction is producing the wrong behavior? The AI proposes 2–3 fix options unless the tester already described how they want it done. Surface the tradeoffs — not just the recommendation.
Find the cause, not the symptom
›
Step 03
🔪
Brain Surgery
Nothing is touched until the Super User confirms the plan. Once confirmed — execute. Revise only the affected section of the prompt. Not the whole prompt. Show the revised section in isolation so the change is visible and reviewable before integration.
Confirm first. Cut second.
›
Step 04
🔗
Transplant
Integrate the revised section into the complete prompt. Replace the old instruction. Save. The complete revised prompt is shown in full so the Super User can confirm the fix is in the right place and nothing adjacent was disrupted.
Replace. Save. Confirm.
›
Step 05
📡
Post-Op Monitoring
Test the exact scenarios that triggered the original failures. Do not assume it worked — verify. Use the tester's original inputs from the Week 3 log. If the behavior changed correctly, the surgery is complete. If not, return to Diagnose.
Verify. Don't assume.
⚠️
The Confirmation Gate — Between Diagnose and Surgery
After Step 2, the AI proposes a fix. The Super User must explicitly confirm before Step 3 begins. This is the only moment where the session pauses and waits. An unconfirmed surgery on a live system creates new problems while fixing old ones.
🧠
"You are not fixing the bot.
The bot is fixing itself — with your guidance.
Your job is to ask the right question
and validate the output."
— The Live Brain Surgery Principle
